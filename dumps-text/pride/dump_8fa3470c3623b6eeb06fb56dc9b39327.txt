   [202][220px-Allegory_of_Pride_Met_DP888806.jpg] Allegory of pride, from
   c. 1590–1630, engraving, 22.3 cm × 16.6 cm, in the [203]Metropolitan
   Museum of Art (New York City)

   Pride is defined by [204]Merriam-Webster as "reasonable self-esteem" or
   "confidence and satisfaction in oneself".^[205][1] [206]Oxford defines
   it as "the quality of having an excessively high opinion of oneself or
   one's own importance."^[207][2] Pride may be related to one's own
   abilities or achievements, positive characteristics of friends or
   family, or [208]one's country. [209]Richard Taylor defined pride as
   "the justified [210]love of oneself",^[211][3] as opposed to false
   pride or [212]narcissism. Similarly, [213]St. Augustine defined it as
   "the love of one's own excellence",^[214][4] and [215]Meher Baba called
   it "the specific feeling through which [216]egoism manifests."^[217][5]

   [218]Philosophers and [219]social psychologists have noted that pride
   is a complex secondary emotion that requires the development of a
   [220]sense of self and the mastery of relevant conceptual distinctions
   (e.g. that pride is distinct from happiness and joy) through
   language-based interaction with others.^[221][6] Some social
   psychologists identify the nonverbal expression of pride as a means of
   sending a functional, automatically perceived signal of high social
   status.^[222][7]

   Pride may be considered the opposite of [223]shame or of
   [224]humility,^[225][8] sometimes as proper or as a [226]virtue, and
   sometimes as corrupt or as a [227]vice. With a positive connotation,
   pride refers to a content sense of attachment toward one's own or
   another's choices and actions, or toward a whole [228]group of people,
   and is a product of [229]praise, independent [230]self-reflection, and
   a fulfilled feeling of [231]belonging. Other possible objects of pride
   are [232]one's ethnicity, and one's [233]sex identity (for example
   [234]LGBT pride).^[[235]citation needed] With a negative
   [236]connotation pride refers to a foolishly^[237][9] and irrationally
   corrupt sense of one's personal value, [238]status or
   accomplishments,^[239][10] used [240]synonymously with [241]hubris.

   While some philosophers such as [242]Aristotle (and [243]George Bernard
   Shaw) consider pride (but not hubris) a profound [244]virtue, some
   world [245]religions consider pride's fraudulent form a [246]sin, such
   as is expressed in [247]Proverbs [248]11:2 of the [249]Hebrew Bible. In
   Judaism, pride is called the root of all evil. When viewed as a virtue,
   pride in one's abilities is known as virtuous pride, greatness of soul,
   or [250]magnanimity, but when viewed as a vice it is often known to be
   self-[251]idolatry, sadistic contempt, [252]vanity, or vainglory.

Etymology[[253]edit]

   Proud comes from late [254]Old English prut, probably from [255]Old
   French prud "brave, valiant" (11th century) (which became preux in
   French), from [256]Late Latin term prodis "useful", which is compared
   with the Latin prodesse "be of use".^[257][11] The sense of "having a
   high opinion of oneself", not in French, may reflect the Anglo-Saxons'
   opinion of the Norman knights who called themselves "proud".^[258][12]

Ancient Greek philosophy[[259]edit]

   [260]Aristotle identified pride (megalopsuchia, variously translated as
   proper pride, the greatness of soul and magnanimity)^[261][13] as the
   crown of the virtues, distinguishing it from vanity, temperance, and
   humility, thus:

     By a high-minded man we seem to mean one who claims much and
     deserves much: for he who claims much without deserving it is a
     fool; but the possessor of a virtue is never foolish or silly. The
     man we have described, then, is high-minded. He who deserves little
     and claims little is temperate [or modest], but not high-minded: for
     high-mindedness [or greatness of soul] implies greatness, just as
     beauty implies stature; small men may be neat and well proportioned,
     but cannot be called beautiful.^[262][14]

   He concludes then that

     High-mindedness, then, seems to be the crowning grace, as it were,
     of the virtues; it makes them greater, and cannot exist without
     them. And on this account it is a hard thing to be truly
     high-minded; for it is impossible without the union of all the
     virtues.^[263][14]

   By contrast, Aristotle defined the vice of [264]hubris as follows:

     to cause shame to the victim, not in order that anything may happen
     to you, nor because anything has happened to you, but merely for
     your own gratification. Hubris is not the requital of past injuries;
     this is revenge. As for the pleasure in hubris, its cause is this:
     naive men think that by ill-treating others they make their own
     superiority the greater.^[265][15]

   Thus, although pride and hubris are often deemed the same thing, for
   Aristotle and many philosophers hubris is an entirely different thing
   from pride.

Psychology[[266]edit]

   Pride, when classified as an emotion or passion, is both cognitive and
   evaluative; its object, that which it cognizes and evaluates, is the
   self and its properties, or something the proud individual identifies
   with.^[267][10] The field of psychology classifies it with [268]guilt
   and [269]shame as a [270]self-conscious emotion that results from the
   evaluations of oneself and one's behavior according to internal and
   external standards.^[271][16] Pride results from satisfying or
   conforming to a standard; guilt or shame from defying it. There is a
   lack of research that addresses pride, perhaps because it is despised
   as well as valued in the individualist [272]West, where it is
   experienced as pleasurable.^[273][17]

  Emotion[[274]edit]

   In psychological terms, positive pride is "a pleasant, sometimes
   exhilarating, emotion that results from a positive
   self-evaluation".^[275][18] It was added to the University of
   California, Davis, "Set of Emotion Expressions", as one of three
   "self-conscious" emotions known to have recognizable [276]expressions
   (along with [277]embarrassment and [278]shame).^[279][19]

   The term "fiero" was coined by Italian psychologist Isabella Poggi to
   describe the pride experienced and expressed in the moments following a
   personal triumph over adversity.^[280][20] Facial expressions and
   gestures that demonstrate pride can involve a lifting of the chin,
   smiles, or arms on hips to demonstrate victory. Individuals may
   implicitly grant status to others based solely on their expressions of
   pride, even in cases in which they wish to avoid doing so. Indeed, some
   studies show that the nonverbal expression of pride conveys a message
   that is automatically perceived by others about a person's high social
   status in a group.^[281][7]

   Behaviorally, pride can also be expressed by adopting an expanded
   posture in which the head is tilted back and the arms extended out from
   the body. This postural display is innate as it is shown in
   congenitally blind individuals who have lacked the opportunity to see
   it in others.^[282][21]

  Positive outcomes[[283]edit]

   Pride results from self-directed satisfaction with meeting personal
   goals; for example positive performance outcomes elicit pride in a
   person when the event is appraised as having been caused by that person
   alone.^[284][22]^[[285]full citation needed]

   Pride as a display of the strong self that promotes feelings of
   similarity to strong others, as well as differentiation from weak
   others. Seen in this light, pride can be conceptualized as a
   hierarchy-enhancing emotion, as its experience and display helps rid
   negotiations of conflict.^[286][23]

   Pride involves exhilarated pleasure and a feeling of accomplishment. It
   is related to "more positive behaviors and outcomes in the area where
   the individual is proud".^[287][24]^[[288]full citation needed] Pride
   is associated with positive social behaviors such as helping others and
   outward promotion^[[289]clarification needed]. Along with hope, it is
   an emotion that facilitates performance attainment, as it can help
   trigger and sustain focused and appetitive effort to prepare for
   upcoming evaluative events. It may also help enhance the quality and
   flexibility of the effort expended.^[290][25]^[[291]full citation
   needed] Pride can enhance creativity, productivity, and
   altruism.^[292][26]^[[293]full citation needed] Pride is associated
   with a higher [294]GPA in less socioeconomically advantaged
   neighborhoods, whereas in more advantaged neighborhoods, pride is
   associated with a lower GPA.^[295][27]

  Economics[[296]edit]

   In the field of [297]economic psychology, pride is conceptualized on a
   spectrum ranging from "proper pride", associated with genuine
   achievements, and "false pride", which can be maladaptive or even
   pathological. Lea et al. examined the role of pride in various economic
   situations and claim that in all cases pride is involved because
   economic decisions are not taken in isolation from one another, but are
   linked together by the selfhood of the people who take
   them^[[298]clarification needed].^[299][28] Understood in this way,
   pride is an emotional state that works to ensure that people take
   financial decisions that are in their long-term interests, even when in
   the short term they would appear irrational.

  Sin and self-acceptance[[300]edit]

   See also: [301]Self-esteem § Contingent vs. non-contingent

   [302][180px-Pride%2C_Jacob_Matham.png] Pride, from the Seven Deadly
   Sins by [303]Jacob Matham c. 1592.

   Inordinate self-esteem is called "pride".^[304][29] Classical Christian
   theology views pride as being the result of high self-esteem, and
   thus^[[305]non sequitur] high self-esteem was viewed as the primary
   human problem, but beginning in the 20th century, "[306]humanistic
   psychology" diagnosed the primary human problem as low self-esteem
   stemming from a lack of belief in one's "true worth". [307]Carl Rogers
   observed that most people "regard themselves as worthless and
   unlovable." Thus, they lack self-esteem.^[308][30]^: 40, 87, 95 

   In the [309]King James Bible, people exhibiting excess pride are
   labeled with the term, [310]"Haughty".

     [311]Pride goeth before destruction, and an haughty spirit before a
     fall.

   — [312]King James Version of the Bible (1611), [313]Book of Proverbs,
   [314]16:18

   Terry Cooper describes excessive pride (along with low self-esteem) as
   an important framework in which to describe the human condition. He
   examines and compares the [315]Augustinian-[316]Niebuhrian conviction
   that pride is primary, the feminist concept of pride as being absent in
   the experience of women, the [317]humanistic psychology position that
   pride does not adequately account for anyone's experience, and the
   humanistic psychology idea that if pride emerges, it is always a false
   front designed to protect an undervalued self.^[318][30]

   He considers that the work of certain neo-Freudian psychoanalysts,
   namely [319]Karen Horney, and offers promise in dealing
   with^[[320]clarification needed] what he calls a "deadlock between the
   overvalued and undervalued self".^[321][30]^: 112–13  Cooper refers to
   their work in describing the connection between religious and
   psychological pride as well as sin to describe how a neurotic pride
   system underlies an appearance of self-contempt and low self-esteem:

     The "idealized self," the "tyranny of the should," the "pride
     system", and the nature of self-hate all point toward the
     intertwined relationship between neurotic pride and self-contempt.
     Understanding how a neurotic pride system underlies an appearance of
     self-contempt and low self-esteem.^[[322]sentence
     fragment]^[323][30]^: 112–13 

   Thus, [324]hubris, which is an exaggerated form of self-esteem, is
   sometimes actually a lie used to cover the lack of self-esteem the
   hubristic person feels deep down.

  Hubris and group narcissism[[325]edit]

   Main article: [326]Hubris

   See also: [327]Group narcissism

   Hubris is associated with more intra-individual negative outcomes and
   is commonly related to^[[328]clarification needed] expressions of
   aggression and hostility.^[329][31]^[[330]full citation needed] Hubris
   is not necessarily associated with high [331]self-esteem but with
   highly fluctuating or variable self-esteem. Excessive feelings of
   hubris have a tendency to create conflict and sometimes to terminate
   close relationships, which has led it to be understood as one of the
   few emotions with no clear positive or adaptive
   functions.^[332][32]^[[333]full citation needed]

   A group that boasts, gloats, or denigrates others tends to become a
   group with low social status or to be vulnerable to threats from other
   groups.^[334][33]^[[335]better source needed] "[H]ubristic, pompous
   displays of group pride might be a sign of group insecurity as opposed
   to a sign of strength,"^[[336]This quote needs a citation] while those
   who express pride by being filled with [337]humility whilst focusing on
   members' efforts and hard work tend to achieve high social standing in
   both the adult public and personal eyes.

   Research from the [338]University of Sydney found that hubristic pride
   correlates with arrogance and self-aggrandizement and promotes
   [339]prejudice and discrimination. But authentic pride is associated
   with self-confidence and accomplishment and promotes more positive
   attitudes toward outgroups and stigmatized individuals.^[340][34]

  Across the world[[347]edit]

   Pride in ones own ethnicity or ones own culture seems to universally
   have positive connotations,^[[348]dubious – [349]discuss]^[350][35]
   though like earlier discussions on pride, when pride tips into hubris,
   people have been known to commit atrocities.^[351][36]

   Types of pride across the world seem to have a broad variety. The
   difference of type may have no greater contrast than that between the
   U.S. and China.^[[352]ambiguous]^[353][37] In the U.S., individual
   pride tends^[[354]clarification needed] and seems to be held more often
   in thought. The people in China seem to hold greater
   views^[[355]clarification needed] for the nation as a whole.^[356][38]

   The value of pride in the individual or the society as a whole seems to
   be a running theme and debate among cultures.^[357][39] This debate
   shadows the discussion on pride so much so that perhaps the discussion
   on pride shouldn't be about whether pride is necessarily good or bad,
   but about which form of it is the most useful.^[358][39]

   Pride has gained a lot of negative recognition in the western cultures
   largely due to its status as one of the Seven Deadly Sins. It was
   popularized by the Pope Gregory I of the Catholic Church in the late
   sixth century, but before that it was recognized by a Christian Monk
   named Evagrius Ponticus in the fourth century as one of the evils human
   beings should resist. ^[359][40]

  German[[360]edit]

   The Father and Mother by [361]Boardman Robinson depicting [362]War as
   the [363]offspring of [364]Greed and Pride.

   Main article: [365]German nationalism

   In [366]Germany, "national pride" ("Nationalstolz") is often associated
   with [367]Nazism. Strong displays of national pride are therefore
   considered to be in poor taste by many Germans. There is an ongoing
   public debate about the issue of German [368]patriotism. The [369]World
   Cup in 2006, held in Germany, saw a wave of patriotism sweep the
   country in a manner not seen for many years. Although many were
   hesitant to show such blatant support as the hanging of the national
   flag from windows, as the [370]team progressed through the tournament,
   so too did the level of support across the nation.^[371][41]

  Asian[[372]edit]

   Main article: [373]Asian pride

   The term "[374]Asian pride" in modern usage refers mostly to those of
   [375]East Asian descent, though it can include anyone of [376]Asian
   descent. Asian pride was originally fragmented, as Asian nations have
   long had conflicts with each other; examples are the old Japanese and
   Chinese religious beliefs about their superiority. Asian pride emerged
   prominently during European [377]colonialism.^[378][42] At one time,
   Europeans controlled 85% of the world's land through colonialism,
   resulting in [379]anti-Western feelings among Asian nations.^[380][42]
   Today, some Asians still look upon European involvement in their
   affairs with suspicion.^[381][42] In contrast, Asian [382]empires are
   proudly remembered by adherents of Asian Pride.

  Black[[383]edit]

   Main article: [384]Black pride

   [385]Black pride is a slogan used primarily in the United States to
   raise awareness for a black racial identity. The slogan has been used
   by [386]African Americans of sub-Saharan African origin or ancestry to
   denote a feeling of self-confidence, self-respect, celebrating one's
   heritage, and being proud of one's worth.

  White[[387]edit]

   Main article: [388]White pride

   [389]White pride is a slogan mainly (but not exclusively) used by
   [390]white separatist, [391]white nationalist, [392]neo-Nazi, and
   [393]white supremacist organizations in the [394]United States for a
   [395]white [396]race identity.^[397][43]^[[398]full citation needed]
   White pride also consists of white ethnic/cultural pride.

Mad[[399]edit]

   Main article: [400]Mad pride

   [401][180px-2016-05-16-Mad_Pride_K%C3%B6ln_2016-4988.jpg] Bed Push at
   Mad Pride parade in Cologne, Germany, in 2016

   Mad pride is a worldwide movement and philosophy that mentally ill
   people should be proud of their madness. It advocates mutual support
   and rallies for their rights,^[402][44] and aims to popularize the word
   "mad" as a self-descriptor.^[403][45]

LGBT[[404]edit]

   Main article: [405]LGBT pride

   [406][180px-2017-05-27_D%C3%BCsseldorf%2C_Christopher_Street_Day_%2810%
   29_%28freddy2001%29_%28cropped%29.jpg] [407]Pride parade,
   [408]Düsseldorf 2017

   [409]LGBT pride is a worldwide movement and philosophy asserting that
   [410]lesbian, [411]gay, [412]bisexual, [413]transgender, and [414]queer
   ([415]LGBTQ+) individuals should be proud of their [416]sexual
   orientation and [417]gender identity. LGBT pride includes advocacy for
   equal [418]rights and [419]benefits for LGBT people.^[420][46] The
   movement has three main premises: that people should be proud of their
   [421]sexual orientation and [422]gender identity, that [423]sexual
   diversity is a gift, and that sexual orientation and gender identity
   are inherent and cannot be intentionally
   altered.^[424][47]^[[425]better source needed]

   The word "pride" is used in this case as an antonym for "[426]shame".
   It is an affirmation of self and community. The modern gay pride
   movement began after the [427]Stonewall riots of the late 1960s. In
   June 1970, the first [428]pride parade in the United States
   commemorated the one-year anniversary of the Stonewall riots—the nearly
   week-long uprising between New York City youth and police officers
   following a raid of Stonewall Inn.^[429][48]

Vanity[[430]edit]

   Main article: [431]Vanity

   [432][220px-Jheronimus_Bosch_Table_of_the_Mortal_Sins_%28Superbia%29.jp
   g] Detail of "Pride" in [433]The Seven Deadly Sins and the Four Last
   Things by [434]Hieronymus Bosch

   In conventional parlance, vanity sometimes is used in a positive sense
   to refer to a rational concern for one's appearance, attractiveness,
   and dress, and is thus not the same as pride. However, it also refers
   to an excessive or irrational belief in or concern with one's abilities
   or attractiveness in the eyes of others and may in that sense be
   compared to pride. The term vanity originates from the Latin word
   [435]vanitas meaning emptiness, untruthfulness, futility, foolishness,
   and empty pride.^[436][49] Here empty pride means a fake pride, in the
   sense of vainglory, unjustified by one's own achievements and actions,
   but sought by pretense and appeals to superficial characteristics.

   y_after_1621%2C_NGA_5360.jpg] Jacques Callot, Pride (Vanity), probably
   after 1621

   In many religions, vanity is considered a form of self-[441]idolatry,
   in which one rejects God for the sake of one's own [442]image, and
   thereby becomes divorced from the [443]graces of [444]God. The stories
   of [445]Lucifer and [446]Narcissus (who gave us the term
   [447]narcissism), and others, attend to a pernicious aspect of vanity.

   In Western art, vanity was often symbolized by a [448]peacock, and in
   [449]Biblical terms, by the [450]Whore of Babylon. During the
   [451]Renaissance, vanity was invariably represented as a naked
   [452]woman, sometimes seated or reclining on a couch. She attends to
   her hair with a comb and mirror. The mirror is sometimes held by a
   [453]demon or a [454]putto. Other symbols of vanity include jewels,
   gold coins, a purse, and often by the figure of [455]death himself.
   [456][170px-Allisvanity.jpg] "All Is Vanity" by [457]C. Allan Gilbert,
   evoking the inevitable decay of life and beauty toward death

   Often depicted is an inscription on a scroll that reads Omnia Vanitas
   ("All is Vanity"), a quote from the Latin translation of the Book of
   [458]Ecclesiastes.^[459][50] Although that phrase—itself depicted in a
   type of still life called [460]vanitas—originally referred not to an
   obsession with one's appearance, but to the ultimate fruitlessness of
   man's efforts in this world, the phrase summarizes the complete
   preoccupation of the subject of the picture. "The artist invites us to
   pay [461]lip-service to condemning her", writes [462]Edwin Mullins,
   "while offering us full permission to drool over her. She admires
   herself in the glass, while we treat the picture that purports to
   incriminate her as another kind of glass—a window—through which we peer
   and secretly desire her."^[463][51] The theme of the recumbent woman
   often merged artistically with the non-allegorical one of a reclining
   [464]Venus.

   In his table of the [465]seven deadly sins, [466]Hieronymus Bosch
   depicts a [467]bourgeois woman admiring herself in a mirror held up by
   a devil. Behind her is an open jewelry box. A painting attributed to
   [468]Nicolas Tournier, which hangs in the [469]Ashmolean Museum, is An
   Allegory of Justice and Vanity. A young woman holds a [470]balance,
   symbolizing [471]justice; she does not look at the mirror or the
   [472]skull on the table before her. [473]Vermeer's famous painting
   [474]Girl with a Pearl Earring is sometimes believed to depict the sin
   of vanity, as the young girl has adorned herself before a glass without
   further positive allegorical attributes.^[475][52] All is Vanity, by
   [476]Charles Allan Gilbert (1873–1929), carries on this theme. An
   [477]optical illusion, the painting depicts what appears to be a large
   grinning skull. Upon closer examination, it reveals itself to be a
   young woman gazing at her reflection in the mirror of her [478]vanity
   table. Such artistic works served to warn viewers of the ephemeral
   nature of youthful beauty, as well as the brevity of human life and the
   inevitability of [479]death.
