   Pride is defined by [204]Merriam-Webster as "reasonable self-esteem" or
   "confidence and satisfaction in oneself".^[205][1] [206]Oxford defines
   it as "the quality of having an excessively high opinion of oneself or
   one's own importance."^[207][2] Pride may be related to one's own
   abilities or achievements, positive characteristics of friends or
   family, or [208]one's country. [209]Richard Taylor defined pride as
   "the justified [210]love of oneself",^[211][3] as opposed to false
   pride or [212]narcissism. Similarly, [213]St. Augustine defined it as
   "the love of one's own excellence",^[214][4] and [215]Meher Baba called
   it "the specific feeling through which [216]egoism manifests."^[217][5]

   [218]Philosophers and [219]social psychologists have noted that pride
   is a complex secondary emotion that requires the development of a
   [220]sense of self and the mastery of relevant conceptual distinctions
   (e.g. that pride is distinct from happiness and joy) through
   language-based interaction with others.^[221][6] Some social
   psychologists identify the nonverbal expression of pride as a means of
   sending a functional, automatically perceived signal of high social
   status.^[222][7]

   Pride may be considered the opposite of [223]shame or of
   [224]humility,^[225][8] sometimes as proper or as a [226]virtue, and
   sometimes as corrupt or as a [227]vice. With a positive connotation,
   pride refers to a content sense of attachment toward one's own or
   another's choices and actions, or toward a whole [228]group of people,
   and is a product of [229]praise, independent [230]self-reflection, and
   a fulfilled feeling of [231]belonging. Other possible objects of pride
   are [232]one's ethnicity, and one's [233]sex identity (for example
   [234]LGBT pride).^[[235]citation needed] With a negative
   [236]connotation pride refers to a foolishly^[237][9] and irrationally
   corrupt sense of one's personal value, [238]status or
   accomplishments,^[239][10] used [240]synonymously with [241]hubris.

   While some philosophers such as [242]Aristotle (and [243]George Bernard
   Shaw) consider pride (but not hubris) a profound [244]virtue, some
   world [245]religions consider pride's fraudulent form a [246]sin, such
   as is expressed in [247]Proverbs [248]11:2 of the [249]Hebrew Bible. In
   Judaism, pride is called the root of all evil. When viewed as a virtue,
   pride in one's abilities is known as virtuous pride, greatness of soul,
   or [250]magnanimity, but when viewed as a vice it is often known to be
   self-[251]idolatry, sadistic contempt, [252]vanity, or vainglory.

Ancient Greek philosophy[[259]edit]

   [260]Aristotle identified pride (megalopsuchia, variously translated as
   proper pride, the greatness of soul and magnanimity)^[261][13] as the
   crown of the virtues, distinguishing it from vanity, temperance, and
   humility, thus:
--
     superiority the greater.^[265][15]

   Thus, although pride and hubris are often deemed the same thing, for
   Aristotle and many philosophers hubris is an entirely different thing
   from pride.

Psychology[[266]edit]

   Pride, when classified as an emotion or passion, is both cognitive and
   evaluative; its object, that which it cognizes and evaluates, is the
   self and its properties, or something the proud individual identifies

   evaluations of oneself and one's behavior according to internal and

   lack of research that addresses pride, perhaps because it is despised

  Positive outcomes[[283]edit]

   Pride results from self-directed satisfaction with meeting personal
   goals; for example positive performance outcomes elicit pride in a
   person when the event is appraised as having been caused by that person
   alone.^[284][22]^[[285]full citation needed]

   Pride as a display of the strong self that promotes feelings of
   similarity to strong others, as well as differentiation from weak
   others. Seen in this light, pride can be conceptualized as a
   hierarchy-enhancing emotion, as its experience and display helps rid
   negotiations of conflict.^[286][23]

   Pride involves exhilarated pleasure and a feeling of accomplishment. It
   is related to "more positive behaviors and outcomes in the area where
   the individual is proud".^[287][24]^[[288]full citation needed] Pride
   is associated with positive social behaviors such as helping others and
   outward promotion^[[289]clarification needed]. Along with hope, it is
--
   upcoming evaluative events. It may also help enhance the quality and
   flexibility of the effort expended.^[290][25]^[[291]full citation
   needed] Pride can enhance creativity, productivity, and
   altruism.^[292][26]^[[293]full citation needed] Pride is associated
   with a higher [294]GPA in less socioeconomically advantaged
   neighborhoods, whereas in more advantaged neighborhoods, pride is
   associated with a lower GPA.^[295][27]

  Economics[[296]edit]

   In the field of [297]economic psychology, pride is conceptualized on a
   spectrum ranging from "proper pride", associated with genuine
   achievements, and "false pride", which can be maladaptive or even
   pathological. Lea et al. examined the role of pride in various economic
   situations and claim that in all cases pride is involved because
   economic decisions are not taken in isolation from one another, but are
   linked together by the selfhood of the people who take
   them^[[298]clarification needed].^[299][28] Understood in this way,
   pride is an emotional state that works to ensure that people take
   financial decisions that are in their long-term interests, even when in
   the short term they would appear irrational.

  Across the world[[347]edit]

   Pride in ones own ethnicity or ones own culture seems to universally
   have positive connotations,^[[348]dubious – [349]discuss]^[350][35]
   though like earlier discussions on pride, when pride tips into hubris,
   people have been known to commit atrocities.^[351][36]

   Types of pride across the world seem to have a broad variety. The
   difference of type may have no greater contrast than that between the
   U.S. and China.^[[352]ambiguous]^[353][37] In the U.S., individual
   pride tends^[[354]clarification needed] and seems to be held more often
   in thought. The people in China seem to hold greater
   views^[[355]clarification needed] for the nation as a whole.^[356][38]

   The value of pride in the individual or the society as a whole seems to
   be a running theme and debate among cultures.^[357][39] This debate
   shadows the discussion on pride so much so that perhaps the discussion
   on pride shouldn't be about whether pride is necessarily good or bad,
   but about which form of it is the most useful.^[358][39]

   prominently during European [377]colonialism.^[378][42] At one time,
